# Digital Privacy and Self-Incrimination Amendment

## Proposed Text

The right of the people to be secure in their digital persons, effects, and communications shall not be violated. No person shall be compelled to provide, surrender, or otherwise disclose any password, encryption key, biometric identifier, or other means of accessing digital devices, accounts, or data, nor shall refusal to do so be treated as evidence of guilt or used to draw adverse inference in any legal proceeding. The contents of personal digital devices and accounts shall be treated as an extension of the person for the purposes of the Fifth Amendment's protection against self-incrimination.

## Why This Amendment?

The Fifth Amendment protects individuals from being compelled to testify against themselves. But courts have struggled to apply this 18th-century principle to 21st-century technology, creating a patchwork of contradictory rulings about whether you can be forced to unlock your phone.

Some courts have held that providing a password is "testimonial" and protected by the Fifth Amendment. Others have ruled that biometric unlocks — a fingerprint or face scan — are not testimonial because they don't require you to "say" anything. The result is that the same right may or may not exist depending on whether your phone unlocks with a PIN or a thumbprint.

This distinction makes no practical sense. The contents of a phone — messages, photos, location history, search queries, financial records — are among the most intimate records a person possesses. Compelling someone to unlock access to all of this is functionally identical to compelling them to open their mind for inspection.

Meanwhile, governments around the world are pushing for backdoors in encryption and legal frameworks that treat refusal to decrypt as obstruction. Without explicit constitutional protection, the right to keep your digital life private exists only at the discretion of whichever court happens to hear your case.

This amendment establishes a clear rule: your digital self is your self, and the protections against self-incrimination apply fully.
