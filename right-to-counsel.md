# Right to Informed Legal Counsel Amendment

## Proposed Text

No citizen may be proffered, by any government official or agent, to waive the right to an attorney without having first discussed the fullness of the accusations or charges against them with an attorney representing their interests.

## Why This Amendment?

The Sixth Amendment guarantees the right to counsel. Miranda v. Arizona established that suspects must be informed of this right. But the current system allows — and routinely encourages — people to waive that right before they understand what they're facing.

In practice, suspects are read their rights and immediately asked to waive them, often under pressure, while disoriented, frightened, or unaware of the severity of the charges. Interrogation techniques are specifically designed to obtain waivers quickly, before a suspect has time to think clearly. The result is that the right to counsel exists on paper but is routinely surrendered by people who need it most.

This amendment would close the gap by requiring that a person actually speak with an attorney about their case before they can waive the right to have one. It does not prevent anyone from representing themselves — it ensures that the decision to do so is an informed one.
