# Legislative Compensation Amendment

## Proposed Text

Senators and Representatives shall be compensated no more than ten times the federal minimum wage.

## Why This Amendment?

The 27th Amendment — the most recently ratified amendment to the Constitution — addresses congressional pay, but only requires that pay changes take effect after an intervening election. It does not cap compensation, and Congress retains the power to set its own salary.

Tying legislative compensation to the federal minimum wage creates a direct, structural incentive for Congress to raise the minimum wage. If a senator's maximum pay is ten times the minimum wage, then voting to increase the minimum wage directly increases their own ceiling — and voting to keep it stagnant holds their pay down as well.

At the current federal minimum wage of $7.25/hour, ten times that rate would be $72.50/hour — roughly $150,800 annually for a full-time schedule. This is substantially less than current congressional pay of $174,000, which would create immediate pressure to raise the minimum wage or accept a pay cut.

More fundamentally, this amendment says that the people who write the laws should live closer to the economic reality of the people those laws affect.
