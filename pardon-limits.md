# Limits on Pardons Amendment

## Proposed Text

No President or Executive may use the Pardon Power to pardon or commute the sentence for any offense committed by the Executive, the Executive's family members, or the Executive's business or political associates, nor may the Executive pardon or commute any offense committed by a person not closely associated with the Executive, but who perpetrated the offense to further the Executive's interests in any way. For the purposes of this Amendment, any person employed by the Executive, the Executive's family, or the Executive's business interests, in a personal, political, or business capacity, whether directly or indirectly, is an associate.

## Why This Amendment?

The pardon power exists to serve justice — to correct wrongful convictions, to show mercy where sentences are disproportionate, and to heal national divisions. It was never intended as a tool for self-dealing.

Yet the Constitution places no explicit limits on whom the President may pardon, creating a structural vulnerability: an executive can commit crimes, direct others to commit crimes on their behalf, and then pardon everyone involved — including, potentially, themselves. The question of whether a president can self-pardon has never been resolved by the courts.

This amendment does not eliminate the pardon power. It draws a bright line around the most obvious abuse: you cannot pardon yourself, your family, your employees, or anyone who acted in your interest. The pardon power remains fully intact for its intended purpose — extending mercy to people the executive has no personal stake in.

History has shown that the honor system is insufficient to prevent abuse of this power. Structural limits are needed.
